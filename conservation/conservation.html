Hardy Orchid Society

Conservation

The Hardy Orchid Society is committed to protecting Britain’s wild orchids and promoting responsible cultivation. Our conservation work follows a clear code of practice and a policy designed to safeguard native species while supporting ethical growing.

Conservation Code

1. Wild-collected plants: Members must not buy, sell, or exhibit orchids taken from the wild.

2. Journal submissions: The HOS Journal will not publish articles or advertisements from individuals or organisations known to have illegally dug orchids.

3. Society events: Plants removed from the wild must not be shown or sold at any HOS event.

4. Membership restrictions: Anyone convicted of illegally digging orchids cannot join or renew membership.

5. Rescuing or relocating orchids: Wild orchids must not be moved without proper permission. For non–Schedule 8 species, landowner permission is usually sufficient. Members should consult the Conservation Officer if unsure.

6. Collecting seed: Seed must not be collected without permission. For non–Schedule 8 species, landowner permission is normally required.

Conservation Policy

Conservation Officer: Advises members, liaises with external bodies, and coordinates practical conservation work.

Reducing pressure on wild populations: The HOS encourages propagation from legally obtained material and exchange of surplus propagated plants.

Legal framework: Collecting wild orchid seed without permission is an offence under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 (or Nature Conservation Act 2004 in Scotland). Schedule 8 species require permits from Natural England.

Relocation of threatened orchids: The Society supports relocation when sites are under threat. Members should notify the Conservation Officer and must not relocate plants without permission.

Re-introduction projects: The HOS supports re-introducing native orchids to suitable sites. Members should consult the Conservation Officer before starting any project.

Compliance: The Society opposes any activity that breaches plant legislation. Wild-collected orchids must not be traded or exhibited.

Seed bank donations: Donations must include permission documents or a statement confirming the seed was collected from the sender’s own property.

Examples of Conservation Work

Introduction of common native orchids: Early projects included raising orchids for protected sites such as Dudley Zoo.

Cypripedium calceolus: Members raised seedlings with the Sainsbury Orchid Project at Kew for re-introduction to northern habitats.

Relocation of threatened plants: Thousands of orchids have been relocated from threatened sites.

Roadside Verges

Roadside verges now represent nearly half of Britain’s remaining unimproved grassland and support important orchid populations, including:

  • Pyramidal Orchid
  • Bee Orchid
  • Northern Marsh-orchid
  • Southern Marsh-orchid

Guidance on verge management is available in Verge Maintenance and Orchids.

Schedule 8 Orchids

Collection of seed or samples from these species requires permits from Natural England:

  • Cephalanthera rubra — Red Helleborine
  • Cypripedium calceolus — Lady’s Slipper Orchid
  • Dactylorhiza lapponica — Lapland Marsh Orchid
  • Epipactis youngiana — Young’s Helleborine
  • Epipogium aphyllum — Ghost Orchid
  • Himantoglossum hircinum — Lizard Orchid
  • Liparis loeselii — Fen Orchid
  • Ophrys fuciflora — Late Spider Orchid
  • Ophrys sphegodes — Early Spider Orchid
  • Orchis militaris — Military Orchid
