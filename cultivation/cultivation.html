Hardy orchids depend on symbiotic fungi to germinate. The Society maintains three fungal isolates that have proven effective for a wide range of terrestrial orchids.

Fungi are grown on agar enriched with Vitamin B extract and rolled oats. Once inoculated, the mycelium spreads and is added to sterilised seed to initiate germination.

B1 – Vigorous all‑rounder

Ideal for beginners and often outcompetes contaminants. Effective for many Anacamptis, Dactylorhiza, Goodyera, Gymnadenia, Habenaria, Orchis, Platanthera, Pterostylis, Serapias and Spiranthes species.

A36 – Fast‑growing fungus

Another strong all‑rounder, effective for Gymnadenia, Anacamptis, Dactylorhiza and Spiranthes.

Q414 – Slow‑growing fungus

Less vigorous but successful with Serapias, Dactylorhiza, Spiranthes and most Anacamptis (excluding A. pyramidalis).

Members who achieve germination with additional species are encouraged to inform the Seed Bank Manager so records can be updated.

The Seed Bank Manager is always pleased to receive donations of good‑quality dry seed, provided it is obtained legally.

Legal considerations

  • Seed from plants grown in members’ collections is preferred.
  • Wild‑collected seed must have written permission from the landowner.
  • Schedule 8 species require permission from the relevant statutory agency.

Harvesting

Harvest on a dry day when capsules are at, or just before, splitting. Place capsules in paper bags. Immature capsules cannot be stored in the Seed Bank.

Cleaning

Separate seed from capsule debris using a tea strainer and place the cleaned seed in a paper envelope.

Drying

Drying is essential for long‑term storage. Use oven‑dried rice in an airtight jar (e.g. Kilner jar) and leave seed in the jar for 2–3 days at room temperature before placing it in a sealed glass container for postage.

Sending seed

Avoid franking machines, which crush seed. Use a crush‑proof container (such as a CD box) inside a padded envelope.

Identification

Include good‑quality photographs of the flower and whole plant in bloom. Digital images can be emailed; prints and transparencies can be scanned.

Orchid seeds are extremely small and contain little stored energy. In nature, they rely on infection by a beneficial fungus that supplies carbohydrate and other nutrients.

To encourage more people to propagate hardy orchids, the Society maintains both a Seed Bank and a Fungus Bank for members, offering surplus seed and proven symbiotic fungi.

Home seed sowing

Many members sow seed in “home laboratories” such as kitchens or spare rooms, using improvised sterile workspaces.

Key requirements

  • Sterile working area (glove box or modified fish tank; laminar flow cabinet if available).
  • Pressure cooker to sterilise media and equipment.
  • Agar‑based media, with or without fungi.
  • Jars, Petri dishes, tools, chemicals and agar.

Basic method

  1. Soak seed in sugar solution for 4 days.
  2. Wash three times in sterile water.
  3. Sterilise in thin bleach (1 part bleach to 9 parts water) for 10–30 minutes.
  4. Wash three times in sterile water.
  5. Sow onto the germination medium.
  6. Add fungus if using symbiotic growth.
  7. Seal the vessel and leave to germinate at room temperature in the dark.
  8. Replate protocorms onto fresh sterile medium when large enough to handle.

Orchids for the garden

Several species are suitable for rockeries and borders in British gardens, particularly Dactylorhiza (Marsh Orchids), Epipactis (Helleborines) and Orchis. They prefer soil that is not too heavy and does not dry out in summer.

Raised beds, large pots or patio tubs allow closer attention: use open compost, keep them moist, feed with weak fertiliser and provide shade on hot days. Their flower spikes in June and July are spectacular.

Cold frame / cold greenhouse

With a little protection, many more species can be grown. Winter‑green orchids from Mediterranean regions benefit from a greenhouse or conservatory with a thermostatically controlled fan heater to keep compost just above freezing.
