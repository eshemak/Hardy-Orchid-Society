Symbiotic Seed Germination Fungi

Hardy orchids rely on specific fungi to germinate. The Society maintains three proven isolates that members can use for symbiotic seed germination.

B1 – Vigorous all‑rounder

Effective for many Anacamptis, Dactylorhiza, Goodyera, Gymnadenia, Orchis, Platanthera, Pterostylis, Serapias, Spiranthes. Ideal for beginners.

A36 – Fast‑growing

Suitable for Gymnadenia, Anacamptis, Dactylorhiza, Spiranthes.

Q414 – Slow‑growing

Effective for Serapias, Dactylorhiza, Spiranthes, and most Anacamptis except A. pyramidalis.

Members are encouraged to report additional successes.

Donating Orchid Seed

The Seed Bank welcomes legally obtained, high‑quality dry seed from members.

Legal requirements

  • Seed from cultivated plants preferred
  • Wild seed requires landowner permission
  • Schedule 8 species require statutory permission

Harvesting

Harvest on a dry day when capsules split. Use paper bags.

Cleaning

Separate seed using a tea strainer; store in paper envelopes.

Drying

Dry seed immediately using oven‑dried rice in an airtight jar.

Posting

Use crush‑proof containers; avoid franking machines.

Identification

Include photos of flower and whole plant.

Propagation from Seed

Orchid seeds are tiny and rely on fungal infection for nutrients. Controlled sowing increases success.

Home seed sowing

Members often use improvised sterile setups such as glove boxes.

Requirements

  • Sterile workspace
  • Pressure cooker
  • Agar‑based media
  • Jars, Petri dishes, tools

Basic method

  1. Soak seed in sugar solution 4 days
  2. Wash 3× in sterile water
  3. Sterilise in dilute bleach 10–30 mins
  4. Wash 3× again
  5. Sow onto medium
  6. Add fungus if using symbiotic method
  7. Germinate in dark at room temperature
  8. Replate protocorms when large enough

Growing Hardy Orchids

The Society shares expert knowledge to reduce pressure on wild populations.

Orchids for the garden

Suitable genera include Dactylorhiza, Epipactis, Orchis. They prefer moist, light soil.

Cold frames / greenhouses

Winter‑green Mediterranean species benefit from a thermostatically heated greenhouse.
